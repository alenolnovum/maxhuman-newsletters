Rapamycin in 2026: everything old is new — but the side-effect ledger keeps getting longer

PEARL trial data, weekly dosing rationale, and the immune paradox nobody in longevity Twitter wants to talk about.

Hey crew, Max here.

Rapamycin is the longevity drug the internet has been collectively losing its mind over for a decade. The hype is understandable — the science behind it is genuinely interesting. The self-prescribing trend, however, is a different conversation.

This week we go deep on the PEARL trial (Mannick et al.), the weekly low-dose protocol logic, and what the immune data actually says — including the part where the same drug that might rejuvenate your immune system can also suppress it, depending entirely on how you dose it.

Three things to take away

🥇 The PEARL trial is the most rigorous human data we have — and it's still a relatively small, short study. Know what it actually measured before you cite it.

🥈 Weekly low-dose rapamycin isn't just a cautious workaround. There's a mechanistic rationale for why intermittent dosing might preserve immune benefits while reducing suppression risk.

🥉 The immune story is genuinely paradoxical: rapamycin can look immunosuppressive or immunorejuvenating depending on dose, schedule, and which markers you measure. Both things are true.

HUMAN TRIALS
🧪 What PEARL actually showed — and what it didn't
The 30-second version
PEARL tested low-dose rapamycin analogs in older adults and found immune improvements — but it was a 16-week trial in ~264 people. It is not a lifespan study.

The PEARL trial (Mannick et al., 2018, Science Translational Medicine) randomized 264 adults aged 65+ to placebo or one of three doses of RTB101 (a rapalog — rapamycin analog) for 16 weeks: 10 mg daily, 5 mg daily, or 5 mg + everolimus 1 mg.

The primary endpoint was immune function, specifically influenza vaccine response measured by hemagglutination inhibition titers. The 10 mg daily arm did not improve vaccine response — it actually trended negative. The 5 mg daily arm and the combination arm showed improved vaccine response at week 16, with the combination arm showing a roughly 1.2-fold increase in seroprotection rates versus placebo.

What the trial was and wasn't
  • ✅ What it measured: — Immune response to flu vaccine and infection rates over one respiratory illness season. Not mortality, not biological age, not lifespan.
  • ❌ What it didn't measure: — Long-term safety at these doses, cardiovascular outcomes, cancer incidence, or whether any benefit persists after stopping the drug.
  • 📏 Scale matters: — 264 participants over 16 weeks is genuinely informative for immune markers. It is nowhere near sufficient to make claims about human longevity.
  • 🔬 The mechanistic finding: — Low-dose rapalogs appeared to downregulate TORC1 target genes that are overactive in aging immune cells — specifically genes associated with exhausted or senescent T cells.
The honest read
PEARL is the best human immune-function data we have for rapamycin-class drugs. It's a promising signal in a short trial — not a green light for self-prescribing.

DOSING RATIONALE
📅 Why weekly? The intermittent dosing argument
The core tension
Daily rapamycin at transplant doses crushes immune function. But mTORC1 inhibition may only need to be intermittent to produce longevity-relevant effects — and the off-days matter.

Rapamycin's half-life in humans is approximately 57–63 hours. A single weekly dose of 5–6 mg produces a peak followed by a trough that allows mTORC1 activity to partially recover before the next dose.

The hypothesis — popularized partly by Matt Kaeberlein's Dog Aging Project and by clinicians like Peter Attia — is that this pulsatile inhibition pattern is enough to trigger autophagy upregulation and senescent-cell signaling changes, while giving the immune system enough recovery time to avoid the chronic suppression seen in transplant patients on daily 2–5 mg doses.

The mechanistic logic — unpacked
  • ⚙️ mTORC1 vs. mTORC2: — Chronic daily dosing inhibits both complexes. mTORC2 inhibition is associated with insulin resistance and immune dysfunction. Intermittent dosing may preferentially hit mTORC1 — the longevity-relevant target.
  • 🔄 Autophagy pulse: — Cellular cleanup (autophagy) appears to be upregulated during the inhibition window. You don't need 24/7 inhibition to get this — the pulse may be sufficient.
  • 🐕 Dog Aging Project data: — The ITP and Dog Aging Project used intermittent protocols and showed lifespan extension in mice and cardiac function improvements in dogs. These are not human longevity trials, but they're the strongest mammalian data available.
  • ⚠️ The honest caveat: — The optimal human dose and schedule for longevity (as opposed to immune function) is genuinely unknown. Weekly 5–6 mg is a hypothesis being tested informally by thousands of people right now, not a validated protocol.
Bottom line on dosing
Weekly low-dose is mechanistically coherent — but 'coherent hypothesis' and 'proven protocol' are not the same sentence.
Kaeberlein et al., Dog Aging Project; ITP rapamycin lifespan data, NIA
THE PARADOX
🧬 Immunosuppression vs. immune rejuvenation — both are real
The paradox in one line
The same drug, at different doses and schedules, can either suppress immune function or partially reverse immune aging. The difference is not trivial.

At transplant doses (daily 2–5 mg or higher), rapamycin is a well-established immunosuppressant. It blunts T-cell proliferation, reduces antibody responses, and increases infection risk — that's the whole point when you're preventing organ rejection.

At low intermittent doses, the picture flips. PEARL showed improved vaccine responses. Separate mechanistic work has shown that low-dose rapamycin can reduce the proportion of exhausted PD-1+ CD8 T cells, improve naive-to-memory T-cell ratios, and downregulate inflammatory gene expression in aging immune cells.

Which markers improved — and at which doses
  • 📈 Vaccine seroprotection (PEARL): — Improved in the 5 mg daily and 5 mg + 1 mg everolimus arms. The 10 mg daily arm did not improve — suggesting a dose ceiling where benefit flips to suppression.
  • 🦠 Respiratory infection rates (PEARL): — The combination arm showed a ~50% reduction in infection incidence during the trial period. This is an intriguing finding in a short trial, not a definitive result.
  • 🧫 T-cell exhaustion markers: — Preclinical and early human data suggest low-dose rapamycin reduces PD-1 and p21 expression in aging T cells — markers associated with immune senescence.
  • 🔻 What gets worse at higher doses: — Wound healing, glucose metabolism (rapamycin can impair insulin signaling), lipid profiles, and mucosal immunity. These are not theoretical — they're documented in transplant literature.
The inconvenient truth
The immune-rejuvenation story is real enough to take seriously. The immunosuppression risk is also real enough to take seriously. Anyone telling you only one of these is true is selling something.

SIDE-EFFECT LEDGER
⚖️ The costs — what the ledger actually says in 2026
What we know is growing
The side-effect profile at longevity-adjacent doses is better than transplant doses — but it is not clean. The ledger has real entries on both sides.

Even at weekly low doses, a meaningful subset of users report mouth sores (aphthous ulcers), elevated fasting glucose, delayed wound healing, and elevated triglycerides. These aren't rare edge cases — they're mechanistically expected from mTOR inhibition.

The longer-term unknowns are where things get uncomfortable. We have no randomized human data beyond 16 weeks at longevity-adjacent doses. Cancer risk with chronic low-dose use, cardiovascular effects, and effects on muscle protein synthesis over years — all of this is currently inference from animal models and transplant extrapolation.

The side-effect inventory — honest version
  • 🩸 Glucose and insulin: — Rapamycin impairs insulin-stimulated glucose uptake. Fasting glucose and HbA1c can rise, particularly with more frequent dosing. Monitor if you're metabolically borderline.
  • 💊 Mouth sores: — Aphthous ulcers affect a notable minority of users. Usually manageable, but a consistent enough signal that it shows up in nearly every observational report.
  • 🩹 Wound healing: — mTOR is central to tissue repair. Slowing it down means slower healing. This is not trivial if you're training hard, recovering from injury, or having any procedure.
  • 🫀 Lipid profiles: — Triglycerides can rise. LDL changes are inconsistent across studies. Not universal, but documented enough to warrant baseline and follow-up lipid panels.
  • ❓ The unknowns: — Long-term cancer risk, effects on muscle mass and strength over years, cardiovascular outcomes, and immune function in the context of novel pathogens — none of these are answered by current human data.
The decision framing
If you're a healthy 45-year-old taking weekly rapamycin for longevity, you are in an n=thousands self-experiment with no control group, no long-term follow-up, and no one tracking your outcomes. That's not necessarily a reason not to — but it's the honest framing.

BOTTOM LINE
🎯 Where this leaves us — the signal without the noise
The honest summary
Rapamycin is the most scientifically credible longevity drug candidate in the human pipeline right now. It is also nowhere near ready to be called a proven human longevity intervention.

The ITP has shown lifespan extension in mice across multiple cohorts — including when started late in life. PEARL showed immune improvements in humans at specific doses. The mechanistic case for mTOR inhibition in aging is strong. None of this is nothing.

What's missing is a long-term randomized human trial measuring hard outcomes — mortality, disease incidence, functional decline. The PEARL follow-on work and the ongoing FAME trial are steps in that direction, but results are years away.

How to think about this rationally
  • 🧠 The science is real: — mTOR's role in aging is one of the most replicated findings in geroscience. This isn't supplement-company biology.
  • 📊 The human evidence is thin: — PEARL is 16 weeks, ~264 people, immune markers only. It is a genuinely important study. It is not a lifespan study.
  • 🔁 Dosing is not settled: — Weekly 5–6 mg is the current community consensus for 'low risk, plausible benefit.' It is a hypothesis, not a validated protocol.
  • 📋 If you're going to do this anyway: — Baseline labs before starting — fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel, CBC. Repeat at 3 months. Track wound healing. Don't add other immunomodulatory compounds without understanding interactions.
  • 🚫 Who should not be doing this: — Anyone with active infection, impaired wound healing, metabolic dysfunction, immunocompromise, or who is pregnant. This is not a complete list.
Max's actual take
Rapamycin is the most interesting longevity drug in the room. It is also a drug with a real side-effect profile, no long-term human safety data at these doses, and a community that has collectively decided the animal data is sufficient. Watch the FAME trial. Don't confuse enthusiasm for evidence.

HEALTH HACK OF THE WEEK
🔬 Before you touch any mTOR-modulating drug or protocol: — Get a baseline — fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel. These are the three markers most likely to shift with rapamycin use. If you don't have a baseline, you have no signal. Takes one blood draw.
🥩 If you're interested in autophagy without the drug: — A 16–18 hour fast 2x per week activates mTOR-independent autophagy pathways. Not the same mechanism, not the same magnitude — but zero side-effect ledger and zero prescription required.
