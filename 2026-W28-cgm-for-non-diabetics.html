CGMs for non-diabetics: the honest reckoning

Glucose variability, post-meal spikes, glucotypes — and whether a $300 sensor changes your health or just your anxiety.

Hey crew, Max here.

Continuous glucose monitors have gone from ICU equipment to wellness accessory faster than you can say 'Levels ambassador.' Stelo is now OTC. Lingo is marketing itself to cyclists. Half the people in your gym are reading glucose graphs on their phones between sets.

The pitch is seductive: real-time metabolic feedback, personalized food responses, early warning for insulin resistance. The reality is messier. This week we dig into what the CGM data actually shows for metabolically healthy adults — which metrics matter, which are noise, and whether wearing one changes your biology or just your behavior tracking.

Three things to take away

🥇 Glucose variability metrics like time-in-range and CV do correlate with cardiometabolic outcomes — but the thresholds that matter were defined in diabetic populations, not healthy ones.

🥈 The Stanford 'glucotypes' work showed wild inter-individual variation in post-meal glucose — but most healthy people's spikes, even dramatic-looking ones, still land in a physiologically normal range.

🥉 RCT evidence that CGM-guided eating improves hard outcomes in non-diabetics is thin. Behavior change is real; whether it's durable or clinically meaningful is a different question.

BACKGROUND
📡 What a CGM actually measures — and what it doesn't
The 30-second version
CGMs measure interstitial fluid glucose — not blood glucose directly. There's a 5–15 minute lag, and accuracy degrades at the low and high ends of the range.

Devices like Dexterity's Stelo, Abbott's Lingo, and the sensor behind Levels (currently Dexterity G7 or Libre) all work the same way: a filament sits just under the skin, reading glucose in the fluid between cells every 1–5 minutes.

That interstitial lag matters more than the marketing suggests. During rapid glucose changes — like a spike after a meal — the sensor can be 10–20 mg/dL behind actual blood glucose. You're watching a slightly delayed movie.

What this means in practice
  • ⏱️ Lag is real. — Post-meal peaks on your CGM graph may look scarier or milder than actual blood glucose peaks, depending on the timing of your measurement.
  • 📉 Low-end accuracy is worse. — CGMs are less reliable below ~70 mg/dL. Hypoglycemia readings in healthy adults should be treated with skepticism before you panic.
  • 🔄 Sensor drift happens. — Accuracy degrades over the sensor's 10–15 day life, especially toward the end. Day-12 data is not as clean as day-2 data.

FOUNDATIONAL SCIENCE
🧬 The Stanford glucotypes paper — what it actually found
The finding everyone cites
In 57 non-diabetic adults, post-meal glucose responses to identical foods varied enormously between individuals — but the clinical significance of that variation in healthy people remains genuinely unclear.

The 2018 Hall et al. / Sonnenburg lab Stanford study (Cell) put CGMs on 57 adults — some healthy, some prediabetic — and fed them standardized meals. They identified three 'glucotypes': low, moderate, and severe variability patterns.

The headline was that even 'healthy' people showed dramatically different glucose responses to the same foods. White bread spiked some people more than table sugar. Individual variation dwarfed population averages.

What the paper actually proved vs. what people claim it proved
  • ✅ Proved: inter-individual variation is large. — Your glucose response to a banana is genuinely not the same as your colleague's. That part is real and replicable.
  • ⚠️ Did not prove: that variation predicts disease in healthy people. — The study wasn't powered or designed to show that higher glucose variability in non-diabetics causes worse long-term outcomes.
  • 🔍 The 'severe variability' group was still mostly normal. — Many participants labeled as high-variability had peak glucose values that a clinician would consider physiologically unremarkable — just higher than their own baseline.
  • 📏 Sample size was 57. — This is hypothesis-generating science. Elegant, important — but not a mandate to buy a CGM based on it alone.

METRICS THAT MATTER VS. NOISE
📊 Which glycemic metrics actually correlate with outcomes
The honest signal-to-noise breakdown
Time-in-range and coefficient of variation have the strongest links to cardiometabolic outcomes — but those links were established almost entirely in diabetic or prediabetic populations.

Time-in-range (TIR) — the percentage of time glucose stays between 70–180 mg/dL — is the metric with the most outcome data behind it. In diabetic populations, higher TIR correlates with lower HbA1c, lower risk of retinopathy, and better cardiovascular markers.

Coefficient of variation (CV) — a measure of glucose fluctuation relative to mean — above 36% is associated with hypoglycemia risk in T1D and worse outcomes in T2D. Mean amplitude of glycemic excursions (MAGE) and standard deviation are related but noisier.

What's signal vs. what's noise for non-diabetics
  • 🟢 Signal: time above 140 mg/dL. — Chronic post-meal excursions above 140 mg/dL — even in non-diabetics — are associated with oxidative stress and endothelial dysfunction in observational data. Worth watching.
  • 🟢 Signal: fasting glucose trend over weeks. — A CGM can reveal a creeping fasting glucose that a once-yearly lab test would miss. That's genuinely useful early detection.
  • 🟡 Probably noise: single post-meal spike height. — A 155 mg/dL peak after a bowl of oatmeal, in an otherwise healthy person, is almost certainly not the emergency your Levels score makes it look like.
  • 🔴 Noise: most of the app-generated 'metabolic scores'. — Levels, Lingo, and similar apps create proprietary composite scores. None have been validated against hard clinical outcomes in non-diabetic populations. They're engagement features, not biomarkers.
  • 🔴 Noise: nocturnal 'dips' in healthy people. — Glucose naturally drops overnight. CGM apps flagging 65 mg/dL at 3am as alarming in a non-diabetic adult are manufacturing anxiety, not insight.
The uncomfortable truth
The thresholds that define 'good' CGM metrics were built for people with diabetes. Applying them to healthy adults is an extrapolation the evidence doesn't yet support.
Battelino et al., Diabetes Care 2019; Danne et al., Diabetes Care 2017
HUMAN TRIALS
🧪 What the RCTs actually show in non-diabetics
RCT scorecard for non-diabetics
CGM-guided eating changes what people eat and reduces post-meal spikes. Whether that translates to better cardiometabolic outcomes at 6–12 months: the evidence is thin and mixed.

The 2023 TREAT trial (Liao et al., JAMA Internal Medicine) randomized 153 adults without diabetes to CGM-guided dietary feedback vs. standard dietary advice for 16 weeks. The CGM group ate fewer refined carbs and had lower post-meal glucose excursions. HbA1c, fasting glucose, and weight did not differ significantly between groups at the end.

A smaller 2024 Stanford study in healthy adults found CGM use reduced time above 140 mg/dL and improved dietary quality scores — but the control group also improved, and the between-group difference shrank at 6 months. Behavior change was real; durability was not.

Reading the RCT evidence fairly
  • 📋 CGMs change behavior. — Across studies, people wearing CGMs consistently eat fewer high-glycemic foods. The feedback loop works. That's not nothing.
  • 📉 Hard outcomes haven't moved. — In the trials done so far, HbA1c, insulin sensitivity, lipids, and weight haven't shown statistically significant improvements over standard dietary advice in non-diabetics.
  • ⏳ Trials are short. — Most CGM RCTs in non-diabetics run 12–20 weeks. Cardiometabolic risk is a decade-scale problem. The absence of evidence over 16 weeks is not evidence of absence over 10 years.
  • 🎯 Who benefits most is still unknown. — People with prediabetes, family history of T2D, or metabolic syndrome may benefit more than genuinely healthy adults. The trials haven't stratified this cleanly yet.
The honest bottom line
CGMs are a behavior-change tool with a plausible mechanism and weak hard-outcome evidence in non-diabetics. That's a different product than the one being marketed.

THE PRODUCTS
🛒 Levels, Stelo, Lingo — what you're actually buying
The product landscape in plain English
Stelo is the first FDA-cleared OTC CGM for non-diabetics. Lingo is Abbott's consumer play. Levels is a software layer on top of existing sensors — you're paying for the app and coaching, not a unique device.

Stelo (Dexterity, cleared 2024) costs ~$99/month for two sensors. It's designed for adults without insulin-requiring diabetes and doesn't require a prescription. The accuracy specs are the same as the G7 it's based on — solid, not perfect.

Lingo (Abbott) uses Libre technology and pairs with an app that gives 'metabolic scores.' Levels isn't a sensor — it's a $200/year membership that connects to a Libre or Dexterity sensor and wraps it in coaching, food logging, and trend analysis.

Before you swipe your card
  • 💡 The sensor is a commodity. — Stelo, Lingo, and Levels all ultimately read interstitial glucose. The differentiation is software, coaching, and UX — not fundamentally different biology.
  • 🧠 App scores are not validated biomarkers. — Levels' 'metabolic score' and Lingo's equivalent are proprietary algorithms. They have not been independently validated against clinical outcomes. They are engagement features.
  • 💰 The cost adds up fast. — At $99–$150/month, a year of CGM use costs more than most people's gym memberships. That's worth naming before calling it 'preventive health.'
  • 🎯 Best use case: a 30-day experiment. — One or two sensor cycles to understand your personal glucose patterns — especially around sleep, stress, and specific foods — is a legitimate use. Indefinite subscription is harder to justify with current evidence.
TAKEAWAYS
🔍 The CGM verdict, condensed
  1. 📡 CGMs measure interstitial glucose with a lag. — The dramatic spike on your app may be 10–20 mg/dL off from actual blood glucose during fast changes.
  2. 🧬 The glucotypes paper was real science, not a CGM ad. — Individual variation in glucose response is genuine — but the clinical stakes of that variation in healthy adults remain unproven.
  3. 🟢 Time above 140 mg/dL and fasting glucose trends are the metrics worth watching. — Everything else — app scores, single-spike heights, nocturnal dips — is mostly noise for non-diabetics.
  4. 📋 RCTs show behavior change, not hard outcome improvement. — People eat better with a CGM. HbA1c, weight, and insulin sensitivity haven't moved significantly in trials done so far.
  5. ⚠️ All clinical thresholds were built for diabetic populations. — Applying 'time-in-range' targets from T1D guidelines to a healthy 35-year-old is an extrapolation the evidence doesn't support.
  6. 🛒 Levels is software, not a sensor. — You're paying for an app and coaching layer on top of a commodity sensor. That's fine — just know what you're buying.
  7. 🎯 The best use case is a short experiment, not a subscription. — 30–60 days of CGM data can teach you something real about your personal patterns. Indefinite use is harder to justify.
  8. 🧪 Longer, better-powered RCTs are needed. — 16-week trials in healthy adults can't tell us much about decade-scale cardiometabolic risk. The field needs longer follow-up.
HEALTH HACK OF THE WEEK
🚶 Walk for 10 minutes after your two biggest meals this week. — Post-meal walking blunts glucose excursions by 20–30% in controlled studies — no sensor required. It's the cheapest CGM intervention you can run.
🥗 Eat protein and vegetables before carbohydrates in mixed meals. — Food order consistently reduces post-meal glucose peaks by 30–40% in human trials. Sequence matters more than most people realize.
😴 Protect your sleep before obsessing over your glucose graph. — A single night of poor sleep raises fasting glucose and insulin resistance measurably the next day. Sleep is the cheapest metabolic intervention on the list.
