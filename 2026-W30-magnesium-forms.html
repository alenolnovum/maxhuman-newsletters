Magnesium forms ranked: glycinate, threonate, citrate, and oxide — what the trials actually show

Glycinate, threonate, citrate, oxide — four forms, very different jobs. Here's what the RCT data actually says.

Hey crew, Max here.

Magnesium is the most over-hyped and under-specified supplement in the longevity space. Everyone tells you to take it. Almost nobody tells you that the form you pick determines whether it reaches your brain, your gut, or your toilet. This week we cut through the form confusion with actual human trial data — not mechanism arguments, not rat studies, not vibes.

Three things to take away

🥇 Magnesium oxide is mostly a laxative — absorption fraction sits around 4%, and it has no meaningful RCT evidence for sleep, cognition, or cramps.

🥈 Magnesium L-threonate is the only form with human RCT evidence for cognitive outcomes, because its L-form is the one that actually crosses the blood-brain barrier.

🥉 Glycinate is the best general-use form for sleep and tolerance — high bioavailability, low GI distress, and the most consistent real-world evidence for subjective sleep quality.

THE BASELINE
🧂 Most people are already low — and the RDA is probably not enough
The uncomfortable starting point
~45% of Americans don't hit the RDA for magnesium from diet alone — and the RDA (310–420 mg/day for adults) was set to prevent deficiency, not to optimize any outcome.

The RDA for magnesium is 310 mg/day for adult women and 400–420 mg/day for adult men. Supplemental doses in the trials that actually showed effects typically ran 200–400 mg of elemental magnesium on top of dietary intake — not instead of it.

That distinction matters. Most supplement labels list the compound weight (e.g., 500 mg magnesium glycinate), not the elemental magnesium content. Magnesium glycinate at 500 mg delivers roughly 50–70 mg of elemental magnesium — so you often need 3–4 capsules to hit a therapeutic dose.

How to read a magnesium label without getting fooled
  • 🔢 Check elemental magnesium, not compound weight. — The number on the front of the bottle is almost never the elemental dose. Look for 'elemental magnesium' in the supplement facts panel.
  • 📐 RDA is a floor, not a target. — 310–420 mg/day prevents deficiency. Most trials showing sleep or cognitive effects used 200–400 mg supplemental elemental magnesium on top of a normal diet.
  • ⚠️ Serum magnesium is a bad deficiency marker. — Only ~1% of body magnesium is in blood. Serum levels stay 'normal' until you're severely depleted. Red blood cell magnesium is more informative but rarely ordered.

BIOAVAILABILITY
📊 Absorption fractions by form — the numbers the label doesn't print
Form-by-form absorption reality check
  • 🟢 Magnesium glycinate: ~40–50% absorption fraction. — Absorbed via amino acid transporters — not just passive diffusion. High bioavailability, minimal laxative effect. The glycine co-passenger may also support sleep independently.
  • 🟡 Magnesium citrate: ~25–30% absorption fraction. — Better than oxide, decent elemental density. Mild osmotic laxative effect at higher doses. The most studied form for bowel prep and kidney stone prevention — not for cognition.
  • 🔵 Magnesium L-threonate: absorption fraction similar to glycinate, but the story is tissue targeting, not gut absorption. — The threonate carrier is specifically transported across the blood-brain barrier. Rat studies showed a 15% increase in cerebrospinal fluid magnesium vs. other forms. The L-form matters — D-threonate does not replicate this.
  • 🔴 Magnesium oxide: ~4% absorption fraction. — Cheap, high elemental density on paper, nearly useless systemically. Its main effect is osmotic — it draws water into the colon. Fine as a laxative. Not a magnesium supplement in any meaningful sense.
  • ⚪ Magnesium malate and taurate: absorption data is limited, mechanisms are plausible. — Malate may support energy metabolism (malate is a Krebs cycle intermediate). Taurate has cardiovascular mechanism arguments. Human RCT data for either is thin.
Schuette SA et al., JPEN 1994; Lindberg JS et al., J Am Coll Nutr 1990; Slutsky I et al., Neuron 2010
SLEEP TRIALS
😴 Glycinate vs. oxide for sleep — one has RCT data, one has marketing
The sleep RCT that actually counts
A 2012 RCT in 46 older adults found 500 mg/day magnesium (as oxide) improved sleep time, sleep efficiency, and early morning awakening scores — but the absorption fraction is so low that researchers suspect the effect may partly reflect the small amount that did absorb, not a meaningful systemic dose.

The most-cited sleep RCT used magnesium oxide — which is ironic, given its terrible bioavailability. A 2021 meta-analysis of 7 RCTs (n=1,301) found magnesium supplementation modestly improved subjective sleep quality (SMD −0.414, 95% CI −0.685 to −0.143), with effects strongest in older adults and people with baseline deficiency.

Glycinate-specific sleep RCTs are fewer but mechanistically cleaner. Glycine itself (3 g before bed) has its own RCT evidence for sleep onset and subjective sleep quality — so glycinate may be delivering a two-for-one: magnesium repletion plus glycine's independent sleep effects via NMDA receptor modulation and core body temperature lowering.

What the sleep evidence actually supports
  • ✅ Magnesium supplementation improves sleep in deficient and older adults. — The meta-analytic signal is real, though effect sizes are modest. Not a sleeping pill. A correction for a deficit.
  • ✅ Glycinate is the preferred form for sleep. — Better absorbed, lower GI side effects, and glycine co-delivery may add independent benefit. Typical supplemental dose: 200–400 mg elemental magnesium (~400–800 mg glycinate compound) taken 30–60 minutes before bed.
  • ⚠️ Oxide 'works' in some trials but the mechanism is murky. — Possibly a threshold effect from the tiny fraction that absorbs. Not the form to choose if you have options.
  • ❌ Magnesium is not a first-line insomnia treatment. — If you have clinical insomnia, CBT-I has far stronger RCT evidence. Magnesium corrects a substrate deficit — it doesn't fix the behavioral and cognitive drivers of insomnia.

COGNITION TRIALS
🧠 Threonate: the only form with a plausible brain-delivery mechanism — and a human RCT to back it
The landmark threonate RCT
A 2016 RCT (n=44, ages 50–70) found magnesium L-threonate at 1.5–2 g/day improved composite cognitive scores by the equivalent of ~9 years of brain-age reversal over 12 weeks — though sample size was small and the study was partly industry-funded.

The mechanism is specific: L-threonate (not D-threonate) is transported across the blood-brain barrier, raising cerebrospinal fluid magnesium concentrations in a way that other forms don't replicate. Higher brain magnesium supports synaptic plasticity via NMDA receptor density — the molecular substrate of learning and memory.

A 2023 follow-up RCT (n=109, ages 18–65 with self-reported cognitive concerns) found magnesium L-threonate improved executive function and attention at 30 days, with effects persisting at 60 days. Caveats: subjective outcome measures, no placebo-controlled blinding verification, and again, industry involvement.

Threonate — calibrated expectations
  • 🧪 The BBB-crossing mechanism is real and replicated in animal models. — The L-form specificity is not marketing — it reflects actual transporter biology. D-threonate does not have the same effect.
  • 📊 Human RCT data exists but is limited and partly industry-funded. — Two positive RCTs is a start, not a conclusion. Treat this as 'promising, not proven' for cognition.
  • 💰 Threonate is 3–5x more expensive per elemental mg than glycinate. — If your goal is general magnesium repletion, glycinate is more cost-effective. Threonate's premium is justified only if the cognitive target is the specific goal.
  • 🎯 Typical effective dose: 1.5–2 g magnesium L-threonate compound (~144–200 mg elemental magnesium). — This is lower in elemental terms than a sleep dose of glycinate. You may need to combine forms to hit both brain and systemic targets.

CRAMPS & PERFORMANCE
🦵 Citrate for cramps — the evidence is more complicated than the marketing
The cramp trial verdict
A 2017 Cochrane review of 5 RCTs found magnesium supplementation did not significantly reduce exercise-associated muscle cramps in healthy adults — but did show modest benefit in pregnancy-related leg cramps.

Exercise-associated muscle cramps are now understood to be primarily neuromuscular in origin — not a simple electrolyte deficit. Magnesium deficiency can cause cramps, but most exercising adults cramping during training are not deficient. The supplement is correcting the wrong problem.

Where magnesium citrate does have solid evidence: kidney stone prevention (it binds urinary oxalate) and constipation relief (osmotic effect). These are legitimate clinical uses. Cramps in non-deficient athletes? The evidence is weak.

Cramp claims — sorted by evidence quality
  • ✅ Pregnancy leg cramps: modest RCT support. — Three RCTs show benefit. Likely reflects genuine magnesium demand increase during pregnancy.
  • ✅ Cramps in confirmed magnesium deficiency: yes, obviously. — Correcting a deficiency fixes deficiency symptoms. This is not a supplement argument — it's a repletion argument.
  • ❌ Exercise cramps in healthy, non-deficient athletes: evidence is weak. — The Cochrane review found no significant effect. Neuromuscular fatigue and altered spinal reflex activity are better explanations than magnesium status.
  • ⚠️ Nocturnal leg cramps: mixed evidence. — Two small RCTs showed benefit; two did not. Effect size where present was modest. Probably worth trying if deficiency is suspected — not a guaranteed fix.

THE VERDICT
⚖️ The ranked decision — which form for which goal
Form → target → evidence grade
  • 🥇 Glycinate → sleep, general repletion, GI tolerance. — Best all-rounder. ~40–50% absorption, low side effects, glycine co-benefit for sleep. Start here for 90% of people. Dose: 200–400 mg elemental magnesium before bed.
  • 🥈 L-Threonate → cognitive targets specifically. — Only form with human RCT evidence for brain outcomes. Worth the premium if that's the explicit goal. Dose: 1.5–2 g compound (~144–200 mg elemental). Stack with glycinate if you also want sleep benefits.
  • 🥉 Citrate → bowel regularity, kidney stone prevention, budget option. — Decent absorption (~25–30%), mild laxative effect at higher doses. Good for people who need a cheaper option and can tolerate some GI loosening.
  • 🚫 Oxide → skip it. — ~4% absorption. Cheap for a reason. Only legitimate use is as a laxative. If your supplement contains oxide as the primary form, you're paying for something that mostly goes down the toilet.
  • 🔬 Malate / taurate → mechanistically interesting, evidence-thin. — Plausible for energy metabolism (malate) and cardiovascular support (taurate). Not enough human RCT data to rank confidently. Not the first choice for any specific outcome.

TAKEAWAYS
🔍 Key Takeaways This Week
  1. 🧂 The form is the whole game. — Magnesium oxide and magnesium glycinate are not interchangeable — they behave like different supplements in the body.
  2. 📉 ~45% of people don't hit the RDA from diet. — This is a real, widespread deficit — not a supplement-industry fiction. But the RDA is a floor, not a performance target.
  3. 🧠 Threonate is the only form with brain-delivery evidence. — The L-form specifically crosses the blood-brain barrier. Two positive human RCTs exist — promising, not proven.
  4. 😴 Glycinate is the best sleep form. — High absorption plus glycine's independent sleep-onset effects make it the mechanistically cleanest choice for nighttime use.
  5. 🚽 Oxide is a laxative, not a supplement. — 4% absorption fraction. If it's in your stack, you're mostly paying for a bowel movement.
  6. 🦵 Magnesium doesn't reliably fix exercise cramps in non-deficient adults. — Cochrane review found no significant effect. Neuromuscular fatigue is a better explanation than magnesium status for most athletes.
  7. 💊 Label math matters. — Most labels show compound weight, not elemental magnesium. You often need 3–4 capsules of glycinate to hit a therapeutic elemental dose.
  8. 🔀 Stacking forms is legitimate. — Threonate for brain + glycinate for sleep is a coherent strategy. They're not competing — they're targeting different tissues.
HEALTH HACK OF THE WEEK
🏷️ Flip your magnesium bottle and find 'elemental magnesium' in the supplement facts. — If it says oxide as the primary form or doesn't list elemental content, it's probably underdelivering. Replace it with glycinate if sleep or general repletion is the goal.
🌙 Take 200–400 mg elemental magnesium glycinate 30–60 minutes before bed for 4 weeks. — That's the dose range where the sleep RCT signal lives. Give it a full month — magnesium repletion takes time, not just one night.
🥬 Add one magnesium-dense food daily: pumpkin seeds (156 mg/oz), dark chocolate (64 mg/oz), or cooked spinach (78 mg/cup). — Supplements correct a deficit. Food maintains it. Both matter.
