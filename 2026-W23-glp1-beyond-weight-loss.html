CARDIOVASCULAR OUTCOMES
❤️ SELECT: The Heart Trial That Changed the Conversation
The 30-second version
SELECT enrolled 17,604 adults with obesity or overweight + established cardiovascular disease but NO diabetes. Semaglutide 2.4 mg reduced the primary endpoint — MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events: CV death, non-fatal MI, non-fatal stroke) — by 20% relative risk reduction over ~33 months.

This was a pre-specified primary endpoint, not a post-hoc fishing expedition. The hazard ratio landed at 0.80 (95% CI 0.72–0.90, p<0.001). That's not a rounding error. The trial was specifically designed to answer whether the CV benefit was real and independent of glucose control — and the answer was yes.

What makes SELECT unusual: the population had no diabetes. Previous GLP-1 CV trials (LEADER, SUSTAIN-6, PIONEER 6) all enrolled diabetic patients, so the glucose-lowering effect was always a confounder. SELECT stripped that away. The mechanism debate — is it weight loss, anti-inflammation, direct cardiac effect? — is still open, but the outcome data is not.

What this means in practice
  • The CV benefit appears to exist beyond blood sugar management — this is not just a diabetes drug with a side hustle.
  • MACE reduction of 20% is clinically meaningful, but absolute risk reduction was ~1.5 percentage points over 3 years — context matters when weighing against cost and side effects.
  • Don't extrapolate to people without established CVD. SELECT enrolled high-risk patients. The data doesn't extend to low-risk prevention yet.
The endpoint distinction
MACE was the primary pre-specified endpoint. Individual components (CV death, MI, stroke) were secondary. The primary held. That's the signal.
SELECT Trial — NEJM 2023; Lincoff et al. | N=17,604 | Primary endpoint: MACE
KIDNEY OUTCOMES
🫘 FLOW: The First GLP-1 Kidney Trial to Hit Its Primary Endpoint
Why this is a big deal
FLOW is the first dedicated kidney outcomes trial for a GLP-1 agonist. Semaglutide 1.0 mg reduced the primary composite kidney endpoint by 24% (HR 0.76, 95% CI 0.66–0.88) in people with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease.

The primary composite endpoint in FLOW included: kidney failure (dialysis, transplant, or eGFR <15), sustained ≥50% eGFR decline, and kidney or CV death. That's a hard endpoint — not a surrogate biomarker. The trial was stopped early due to efficacy, which is either exciting or a reason for mild statistical caution depending on your epistemological mood.

The CV death component also drove separation, which muddies the pure nephroprotection story slightly — but the kidney-specific components held up independently in pre-specified analyses. SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) already had kidney outcome data; FLOW now puts semaglutide in that conversation.

What this means in practice
  • FLOW enrolled people with T2D AND CKD — don't generalize to non-diabetic kidney disease without further trial data.
  • The trial was stopped early (median ~3.4 years). Early stopping tends to inflate effect sizes. The direction is clear; the exact magnitude may settle lower.
  • SGLT2i + GLP-1 combination therapy for CKD is now an active clinical question. Trials are running. Watch this space.
Primary vs. exploratory
The composite kidney/CV endpoint was primary and pre-specified. Albuminuria reduction was a secondary endpoint. eGFR slope analyses were exploratory. Hierarchy matters.
FLOW Trial — NEJM 2024; Perkovic et al. | N=3,533 | Primary endpoint: kidney composite + CV death
ADDICTION & CRAVING DATA
🧠 The Addiction Signal: Genuinely Interesting, Genuinely Preliminary
The honest headline
GLP-1 receptors are expressed in dopamine-rich reward areas of the brain. Animal studies and early human data suggest semaglutide and liraglutide reduce craving and consumption of alcohol, nicotine, and opioids — but no large RCT has tested addiction as a primary endpoint yet.

The mechanistic story is plausible: GLP-1 receptors sit in the nucleus accumbens and ventral tegmental area — core reward circuitry. In rodent models, GLP-1 agonists consistently reduce self-administration of alcohol, cocaine, and nicotine. Human observational data from large insurance databases (notably a 2023 study in Nature Communications, ~80,000 patients) found lower rates of alcohol use disorder in semaglutide users vs. comparators — but that's retrospective and confounded.

The SURMOUNT and STEP trial programs were not designed to test addiction endpoints, so what we have are exploratory analyses and secondary reports — not primary outcomes. Small dedicated RCTs in alcohol use disorder are now running (NCT05895643 among others). The signal is real enough to study seriously. It is not real enough to prescribe against yet.

What this means in practice
  • Anecdotal reports of reduced alcohol craving from patients on semaglutide are consistent with the mechanism — but anecdote is not trial data.
  • The retrospective database studies are hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory. Confounding by indication is a real problem in that literature.
  • Dedicated addiction RCTs with pre-specified primary endpoints are the only way to settle this. Those results are likely 2–3 years away.
The endpoint distinction
Addiction/craving findings to date are exploratory secondary analyses or observational data. Zero large RCTs have listed addiction as a primary endpoint. That's the honest line.
Key refs: Klausen et al. 2022 (Nat Commun); SURMOUNT-1 exploratory analyses; multiple ongoing RCTs (NCT05895643)
THE MUSCLE PROBLEM
💪 Muscle Loss: The Side Effect That Deserves a Louder Warning Label
The number people skip over
In STEP-1, participants lost ~15% of body weight on semaglutide 2.4 mg. Roughly 38–40% of that weight loss was lean mass — not fat. That's not a rounding error. That's a significant chunk of muscle.

Lean mass loss during GLP-1-driven weight loss is not unique to this drug class — it happens with any large caloric deficit. But the speed and magnitude of weight loss with semaglutide and tirzepatide amplifies the problem. The SURMOUNT-1 trial (tirzepatide) showed similar lean mass loss proportions. Muscle is metabolically expensive tissue; losing it accelerates the functional decline that longevity researchers spend careers trying to prevent.

The field is actively studying co-interventions: resistance training during GLP-1 treatment consistently attenuates lean mass loss in small trials. Higher protein intake (~1.6g/kg/day) is the other lever. Emerging data on combining GLP-1s with selective androgen receptor modulators or even growth hormone secretagogues exists — but that's mostly preclinical or very small human studies. The practical answer right now is boring: lift weights, eat protein, don't just coast on the drug.

What this means in practice
  • Anyone on a GLP-1 agonist without a resistance training protocol is leaving a significant problem unaddressed. The drug does not protect muscle.
  • Protein targets matter: aim for ≥1.2–1.6g/kg body weight/day during active weight loss on GLP-1 therapy based on current evidence.
  • The 'rebound' risk after discontinuation is partly a muscle story — less lean mass means lower resting metabolic rate means faster fat regain. This is a systems problem.
The bottom line
GLP-1 agonists don't selectively burn fat. Protecting muscle requires active effort — the drug won't do it for you.
STEP-1 (Wilding et al., NEJM 2021); SURMOUNT-1 (Jastreboff et al., NEJM 2022); body composition sub-studies
