Zone 2 cardio: is it actually different, or just rebranded slow jogging?

The metabolic case for boring cardio — lactate thresholds, mitochondria, and what the HIIT vs. Zone 2 data actually says.

Hey crew, Max here!

Zone 2 is everywhere right now. Podcasters won't shut up about it. Coaches are selling it. Your friend who bought a Garmin last year will not stop mentioning their "aerobic base." And like most things that go viral in the wellness space, it's worth asking: is there real science underneath, or is this just slow jogging with better branding?

This week we dig into the actual metabolic case — lactate thresholds, mitochondrial biogenesis, the Iñigo San Millán framework, and the head-to-head data against HIIT. Spoiler: the science is more interesting than the hype, and the practical answer is less either/or than the internet wants it to be.

Three things to take away

🥇 🔬 Zone 2 has a real, specific metabolic mechanism — it's not just slow jogging with a fancier name. The lactate threshold and mitochondrial biology are genuinely distinct.

🥈 ⚔️ HIIT beats Zone 2 for VO2max gains in less time. Zone 2 beats HIIT for fat oxidation and mitochondrial density. They are not the same tool.

🥉 💀 For mortality endpoints, the data converges on one uncomfortable truth: the biggest gains come from going from nothing to something. After that, the zone wars matter less than you think.

THE MECHANISM
🧬 What actually happens at Zone 2 intensity
The 30-second version
Zone 2 is the highest intensity at which your body can clear lactate as fast as it produces it — and that specific threshold is where the most interesting mitochondrial adaptations happen.

Lactate is not the villain it was once made out to be. At low intensities, your muscles produce it constantly — and your mitochondria, especially in slow-twitch Type I fibers, shuttle it back in and burn it as fuel. Zone 2 sits right at the top of this clearing capacity, sometimes called LT1 (the first lactate threshold), where blood lactate stays roughly stable, typically between 1.5–2.0 mmol/L.

This steady-state lactate zone turns out to be a potent signal for mitochondrial biogenesis — the process of building new mitochondria. The key driver is PGC-1α, a transcription factor activated by sustained aerobic stress at this intensity. More mitochondria per cell means better fat oxidation, better lactate clearance, and a larger aerobic engine overall.

What this means in practice
  • 🗣️ Zone 2 is roughly the intensity where you can hold a full conversation but wouldn't want to sing. If you're gasping, you've left the zone.
  • 📊 The most precise way to find LT1 is a lactate test with a sports physiologist. The second-best proxy is a metabolic cart measuring fat vs. carb oxidation.
  • ⌚ Heart rate zones on wearables are rough estimates — they can be off by 10–20 bpm for a given individual. Don't treat them as gospel.
Mechanism grounded in: Iñigo San Millán & George Brooks, Nutrients 2018; Brooks, Cell Metabolism 2020
THE SAN MILLÁN FRAMEWORK
🚴 Why a Tour de France coach made everyone care about going slow
The key argument
Iñigo San Millán's work with elite cyclists shows that metabolic flexibility — the ability to burn fat efficiently at moderate intensities — is built almost entirely in Zone 2, and it degrades sharply with sedentary aging.

San Millán, who has coached Tour de France winners and published extensively on metabolic health, argues that most people — including recreational athletes — spend almost no time at true Zone 2. They go too easy on easy days and not hard enough on hard days, landing in a metabolic no-man's-land that trains nothing particularly well.

His clinical work also links poor Zone 2 capacity to metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, and type 2 diabetes — conditions where mitochondrial dysfunction in muscle is a central feature, not a side effect. The argument isn't just about fitness. It's about whether your cells can handle fuel efficiently at all.

The San Millán checklist
  • 🔥 If your fat-oxidation rate peaks below 0.5 g/min during exercise testing, your Zone 2 capacity is likely underdeveloped — regardless of how fit you feel.
  • 📉 Sedentary adults and people with metabolic dysfunction often have a very compressed Zone 2 range — LT1 occurs at embarrassingly low intensities.
  • 🏗️ The prescription is boring but consistent: 3–4 sessions per week, 45–90 minutes each, at true Zone 2. Not a month of it. Months of it.

ZONE 2 vs. HIIT
⚔️ The head-to-head data: where each one actually wins
What the trials actually show
HIIT produces larger VO2max gains in less time. Zone 2 produces superior fat oxidation and mitochondrial density gains. Neither fully replaces the other.

A 2023 meta-analysis in British Journal of Sports Medicine found that HIIT improved VO2max by roughly 0.51 L/min more than moderate-intensity continuous training over matched training periods in previously inactive adults. That's a meaningful edge for cardiorespiratory fitness, particularly for time-crunched people. HIIT also produces fast gains in cardiac output and peak power.

But Zone 2 has its own lane. Studies on trained cyclists and metabolic patients consistently show greater improvements in maximal fat oxidation (MFO) and mitochondrial enzyme activity — particularly citrate synthase and β-hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase — with sustained low-intensity work versus interval protocols. For people with insulin resistance or poor metabolic flexibility, Zone 2 may be the more relevant intervention.

How to read the comparison honestly
  • 📈 VO2max is the sexiest number in cardio research, but it's not the only one. Fat oxidation capacity and mitochondrial density matter independently for metabolic health and longevity.
  • ⏱️ HIIT wins on time efficiency for VO2max. If you have 20 minutes three times a week, HIIT is probably the better bet for aerobic fitness gains.
  • 🔄 Elite endurance athletes do roughly 80% Zone 2 and 20% high intensity — the so-called polarized model. That ratio is not an accident.
The honest summary
This is not a cage match. The evidence supports both — for different outcomes, different populations, and different goals. Picking a team is a marketing move, not a scientific one.
Bacon et al., BJSM 2013; Milanović et al., Sports Medicine 2015; Achten & Jeukendrup, Sports Medicine 2004
MORTALITY & LONGEVITY ENDPOINTS
💀 What the mortality data actually says — and where it gets humbling
The number that should reset the conversation
Going from sedentary to 150 minutes of moderate activity per week is associated with roughly a 30–35% reduction in all-cause mortality risk. The marginal gains beyond that exist but shrink fast.

Large epidemiological analyses — including the PURE study and the 2022 Lancet meta-analysis on physical activity and mortality — show that the dose-response curve for cardio and longevity is steep at the bottom and flat at the top. The person going from zero to three Zone 2 sessions per week wins enormous mortality benefit. The person debating whether to do 4 or 5 hours is optimizing in the noise.

Where Zone 2 does show up meaningfully in longevity research is through its association with VO2max — a variable with some of the strongest mortality data in exercise science. A 2018 JAMA Network Open study found that low cardiorespiratory fitness was associated with a higher mortality risk than smoking, hypertension, or diabetes in a large clinical cohort. Zone 2 training builds VO2max, albeit more slowly than HIIT. Both paths lead there.

What to actually do with this
  • 🎯 If you're sedentary, the zone you train in matters far less than the fact that you start. Any consistent cardio beats the alternative by a mile.
  • 📊 VO2max is the longevity biomarker with the best mortality data in exercise science. Improving it — by any method — is worth prioritizing.
  • 🧮 For people already training consistently, the polarized approach (mostly Zone 2 + some hard intervals) has the most support for long-term aerobic development.
The bottom line
Zone 2 is real science, not rebranded slow jogging. But the mortality data is a reminder that the biggest enemy is the couch, not the wrong heart rate zone.
Mandsager et al., JAMA Network Open 2018; Strain et al., Lancet 2022; Sallis et al., BJSM 2010
