Mitochondrial quality control: what the supplement stack gets right, wrong, and half-right

Urolithin A has real human data. NR/NMN are complicated. MOTS-c is promising but early. Let's sort the signal from the noise.

Hey crew, Max here.

This week we are going deep on one of the most hyped corners of longevity supplementation — and one of the few where the hype is occasionally justified. Mitochondrial quality control, specifically mitophagy — the process your cells use to identify and destroy worn-out mitochondria before they turn toxic — is real biology with real consequences for muscle aging, metabolic health, and cellular resilience. The supplement industry noticed. As usual, it got about 40% of the story right.

We are going to look at three interventions that get the most airtime: Urolithin A, the NAD precursors NR and NMN, and the mitochondria-derived peptide MOTS-c. One has moved the needle in humans. One converts to NAD+ reliably but has a clinical outcome problem. One is genuinely exciting and genuinely early. Here is what the evidence actually says.

Three things to take away

🥇 🥇 Urolithin A is the only intervention here with replicated human trial data showing improved mitochondrial markers — specifically mitophagy flux, muscle endurance, and mitochondrial gene expression in older adults.

🥈 🥈 NR and NMN reliably raise blood NAD+ levels in humans. The problem is that raising NAD+ in blood does not reliably translate into clinical outcomes — the muscle, metabolic, and cognitive data are mixed at best.

🥉 🥉 MOTS-c is a mitochondria-derived peptide with striking animal data on metabolism and stress resilience. Human trials exist but are small and early. Do not buy it off the internet yet.

BACKGROUND
🔋 Why mitophagy matters — and why it breaks down with age
The 30-second version
Mitophagy is your cells' quality-control system for mitochondria. When it slows with age, damaged mitochondria accumulate, leak reactive oxygen species, and drive inflammation and cellular dysfunction across muscle, brain, and heart.

Mitochondria are not static power plants. They are constantly fusing, dividing, and getting tagged for destruction when they become damaged. The tagging-and-destruction process — mitophagy — depends on proteins like PINK1 and Parkin sensing membrane potential loss and flagging the damaged unit for autophagosomal clearance. In young, healthy cells this runs like a tight operation. In aging cells, it does not.

The downstream consequences of impaired mitophagy are not subtle. Accumulating dysfunctional mitochondria produce excess reactive oxygen species, leak mitochondrial DNA into the cytosol triggering inflammatory pathways (including cGAS-STING), and reduce the cell's overall ATP output. In skeletal muscle, this manifests as the accelerating decline in mitochondrial content and oxidative capacity that characterizes sarcopenia.

What this means before we get to supplements
  • Exercise — particularly endurance and high-intensity interval training — is still the most potent known inducer of mitophagy in humans. No supplement has matched it.
  • Caloric restriction and time-restricted eating activate overlapping autophagy pathways via AMPK and mTOR suppression. This is not wellness mythology; it has mechanistic support.
  • Chronic sleep deprivation impairs mitochondrial quality control. If your sleep is broken, your supplement stack is building on sand.
HUMAN TRIALS
🍇 Urolithin A: the one with actual receipts
The key finding
Urolithin A is the best-evidenced mitophagy-inducing supplement in humans. Multiple trials have moved mitochondrial markers, muscle endurance, and gene expression in the right direction in older adults.

Urolithin A is a postbiotic — a compound produced when gut bacteria metabolize ellagitannins from pomegranates, walnuts, and certain berries. The problem is that only about 30–40% of people have the gut microbiome to produce meaningful amounts, which is the entire commercial rationale for supplementing it directly. The mechanism is well-characterized: UA activates mitophagy via PINK1/Parkin and also upregulates mitochondrial biogenesis genes.

The human data is what separates UA from most of the mitochondria supplement category. A 2022 randomized controlled trial in 66 older adults (Andreux et al., Nature Aging) showed that 1,000 mg/day UA for four months significantly increased skeletal muscle mitochondrial gene expression, improved mitochondrial biogenesis markers, and enhanced muscle endurance on a six-minute walk test versus placebo. A separate trial confirmed increased mitophagy flux biomarkers in blood. These are not surrogate endpoints invented by a marketing team — they are the actual biology the intervention is supposed to target.

What the data actually supports
  • Improved muscle endurance and mitochondrial gene expression in older adults at 500–1,000 mg/day — this is the dose range with human evidence.
  • Increased mitophagy flux markers in blood — meaning the cellular recycling process appears to actually accelerate, not just get talked about.
  • What it has not yet shown: hard clinical outcomes like reduced all-cause mortality, meaningful cognitive improvement, or disease prevention. The trials are promising but short and in relatively small samples.
The honest verdict
Urolithin A has the strongest human mitochondrial evidence of anything in this category. It is not a miracle. It is a credible, mechanism-matched intervention with replicated biomarker data in the population that needs it most.
Key reference: Andreux et al., Nature Aging, 2022; Palu et al., replication trial data
NAD PRECURSORS
⚗️ NR and NMN: they raise NAD+. Then what?
The uncomfortable middle
NR and NMN reliably raise blood NAD+ in humans. That part is settled. Whether higher blood NAD+ translates into better mitochondrial function, muscle health, or metabolic outcomes in humans — that part is not.

NAD+ declines with age in multiple tissues, and this decline is associated with impaired mitochondrial function, reduced SIRT1/SIRT3 deacetylase activity, and compromised DNA repair. NR (nicotinamide riboside) and NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) are both precursors that enter the NAD+ biosynthesis pathway. Multiple human trials — including work from the Brenner lab and the Yoshino lab — have confirmed that oral supplementation raises whole-blood and PBMC NAD+ levels by 40–90% at doses of 300–1,000 mg/day. That biochemistry is not in dispute.

The clinical outcome problem is real though. A 2021 RCT in postmenopausal women with prediabetes (Yoshino et al., Science) found that NMN improved muscle insulin sensitivity and increased expression of genes involved in muscle remodeling — a genuine positive signal. But other trials have failed to show improvements in physical performance, body composition, cognitive function, or cardiovascular markers in older adults. A 2023 meta-analysis of NR trials found no consistent benefit on cardiometabolic outcomes. The honest read: raising NAD+ in blood is not the same as fixing the downstream biology in tissues that matter.

What to make of the mixed data
  • The muscle insulin sensitivity finding from Yoshino et al. is the most compelling clinical signal — but it was in a specific population (postmenopausal, prediabetic women) and has not been broadly replicated.
  • NR and NMN have excellent safety profiles in human trials. The issue is not harm — it is the gap between a reliable biochemical effect and a reliable clinical benefit.
  • If you are taking NR or NMN hoping to improve mitochondrial quality control specifically, the mitophagy evidence is much weaker than for Urolithin A. NAD+ supports mitochondrial function broadly but does not directly activate the PINK1/Parkin recycling machinery.
The decision
NR/NMN are not snake oil. They are real biochemistry with an unresolved translation problem. The people most likely to benefit are probably those with genuine NAD+ deficiency — older adults, metabolically compromised individuals — not healthy 35-year-olds optimizing from a high baseline.
Key references: Yoshino et al., Science 2021; Brenner et al. NR pharmacokinetics; 2023 NR meta-analysis, Ageing Research Reviews
EMERGING SCIENCE
🧬 MOTS-c: mitochondria's own signaling peptide — and why it's not ready for your stack
What makes this interesting
MOTS-c is a peptide encoded in mitochondrial DNA — not nuclear DNA — that acts as a systemic metabolic regulator. In animal models it improves insulin sensitivity, reduces obesity, and extends lifespan. In humans, plasma MOTS-c levels decline with age.

MOTS-c (mitochondrial open reading frame of the 12S rRNA type-c) was identified by the Lee lab at USC and belongs to a class called mitochondria-derived peptides (MDPs). It activates AMPK, improves glucose uptake in skeletal muscle, and appears to function as a mitokine — a signal that mitochondria send to the rest of the body to coordinate metabolic stress responses. In aged mice, exogenous MOTS-c administration improved physical performance, metabolic flexibility, and stress resilience. The animal data is genuinely striking.

The human picture is early. Circulating MOTS-c levels are lower in older adults and in people with type 2 diabetes, which is consistent with the hypothesis. A small human trial published in 2023 showed that MOTS-c levels increase acutely with exercise, supporting its role as an exercise-responsive mitokine. But no large, adequately powered RCT has tested exogenous MOTS-c supplementation for clinical outcomes in humans. The peptide is being investigated in early-phase trials, and the pharmacology of oral peptide delivery remains a legitimate obstacle.

How to think about MOTS-c right now
  • Do not buy injectable or oral MOTS-c from gray-market supplement vendors. There is no established human dosing, no long-term safety data, and no regulatory oversight of what is actually in those products.
  • The science is worth watching. If early-phase trials confirm the animal findings in humans, MOTS-c could become a genuinely novel mitochondrial intervention — not just another NAD+ pathway tweak.
  • The best proxy for what MOTS-c does naturally? Exercise. Acute MOTS-c release is one of the mechanisms by which exercise improves mitochondrial signaling and metabolic flexibility.
The timeline check
MOTS-c is two to three trial generations away from a confident human recommendation. File it under 'watch closely, do not self-experiment recklessly.'
Key references: Lee et al., Cell Metabolism 2015 (discovery); Kim et al., 2022 exercise/MOTS-c; ongoing Phase I/II trials
BOTTOM LINE
📊 The mitochondria supplement scorecard — honest version
The hierarchy that actually exists
Urolithin A > NR/NMN > MOTS-c — ranked by quality of human evidence for mitochondrial outcomes specifically. That ranking could change. Right now, it is what the trials support.

The mitochondria supplement category is unusual in that it contains at least one intervention — Urolithin A — with replicated, mechanism-matched human trial data and at least one — NR/NMN — with solid biochemistry but genuinely unresolved clinical translation. That is better than most supplement categories, which contain mostly noise. It does not mean the stack replaces the basics.

Exercise remains the most potent mitophagy inducer known. Caloric moderation, sleep quality, and metabolic health govern the cellular environment in which any supplement operates. Urolithin A on top of a broken lifestyle is a very expensive bandage. Urolithin A on top of consistent training, adequate protein, and controlled blood sugar is a potentially meaningful addition for adults over 50 — which is, not coincidentally, the population in whom the trials were run.

Practical decision framework
  • Urolithin A: Reasonable to consider at 500–1,000 mg/day if you are over 50, concerned about muscle aging, and already have the lifestyle basics covered. The evidence is not overwhelming but it is real.
  • NR/NMN: Potentially useful in metabolically compromised older adults. For everyone else, the clinical benefit case is weak. If you take it, do not expect it to specifically drive mitophagy — that is not what the evidence shows.
  • MOTS-c: Not ready. Watch the trials. Do not buy it from a website that also sells peptide 'stacks' and uses the word 'protocol' seventeen times.
The thing worth remembering
The supplement industry sells mitochondrial health as a product. Your mitochondria mostly want you to move, sleep, and stop overloading your metabolic machinery. The molecules above are, at best, useful additions to that foundation — not replacements for it.
